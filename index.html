Dr. Jonas Edward Salk

The man who saved millions of people from paralysis and death caused by polio.

Here's a time line of Dr. Jonas Salk's life:

  • 1914 - Born on October 28 in New York City to Jewish immigrant parents.
  • 1934 - Enrolls at the City College of New York to study chemistry, inspired by his mother to pursue higher education.
  • 1939 - Graduates from New York University School of Medicine, where he originally planned to become a practicing physician but later shifts to medical research.
  • 1942 - Begins working at the University of Michigan's School of Public Health with his mentor Dr. Thomas Francis Jr. on developing an influenza vaccine for the U.S. military during World War II.
  • 1947 - Becomes head of the Virus Research Laboratory at the University of Pittsburgh. Starts focusing on poliovirus, aiming to develop a safe and effective vaccine.
  • 1952 - Poliomyelitis cases peak in the U.S., with over 58,000 cases, causing paralysis and death, particularly in children. Salk begins testing his polio vaccine, which uses inactivated (killed) virus, in small groups of children and adults.
  • 1954 - Launches a large-scale national field trial for the polio vaccine, the largest medical experiment in history, involving 1.8 million children, known as "Polio Pioneers.
  • 1955 - April 12: Salk's polio vaccine is declared safe and effective. Mass vaccinations begin, leading to a dramatic drop in polio cases across the U.S. Salk becomes an international hero.
  • 1957 - Receives the Lasker Award for Public Service for his groundbreaking work on the polio vaccine.
  • 1960 - Founds the Salk Institute for Biological Studies in La Jolla, California, an independent research institute designed to explore biological sciences and human well-being.
  • 1963 - Dedicates the rest of his career to research on cancer, multiple sclerosis, and HIV/AIDS in the later years of his life, continuing his lifelong pursuit of curing diseases.
  • 1977 - Polio is declared eradicated in most parts of the world thanks to widespread use of the vaccine.
  • 1981 - Publishes the book World Population and Human Values: A New Reality, where he addresses the need for ethical considerations in science and societal progress.
  • 1984 - Begins working on an AIDS vaccine, aiming to combat the growing epidemic.
  • 1995 - Dies on June 23 at the age of 80 in La Jolla, California. His legacy as a public health hero remains enduring.

“There is no patent. Could you patent the sun?”

- Jonas Salk, when asked who owned the patent for his polio vaccine.
